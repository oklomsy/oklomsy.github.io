What does "Technologically accessible" mean?

Date: Feb 20, 2022

You may have read my blog post about why the Internet sucks, there are so many reasons and I will explain every single one here and also what it means to be technologically accessible.

Present

Right now, most websites contain code or assets from other websites, websites might import fonts from Google Fonts or they might use Google Analytics to collect user metrics. The problem is that these tools slow the web, harm privacy and makes big tech bigger.

Even a simple resource (such as a simple script to show the time or a stylesheet) can harm your user’s privacy, if you have gone through the effort of hosting your own website, then the last thing you want is to nullify all of the benefits by adding external resources.

External resources slow down everything, if you know basic web design then you know that users don’t want to wait more than a couple of seconds for the webpage to load. External resources make this goal impossible.

But even then, your website can be completely functional without external resources and still not be technologically accessible.

If your website uses JavaScript then it is automatically not technologically-accessible unless it’s an optional script, if you force users to enable JavaScript to read your content, then that is an injustice to your users, you are forcing them to run code and possibly identify themselves in an unneccessary manner.

Forget JavaScript! We do not need it! Besides, it harms accessibility when it comes to supporting other browsers. Lots of people use text-browsers (which do not support JavaScript) either out of neccessity or because they don’t want to use a memory-inefficient “web browser”

By adding JavaScript, you are harming those users and potentially leaving them dissatisfied. I am not saying you’re not allowed to use JavaScript, I am saying that you should keep it simple.

Do you really need all of that?

In this age, you should be asking yourself, how can I design technologically accessible websites? Websites that work in any environment, every browser should be supported and the easiest way to do that is to throw out technologically inaccessible elements and features such as JavaScript or remote fonts.

Text browsers, phones and phones running text browsers should be supported, every user should be able to browse your site without it looking ugly and the only way to do that is to think critically of how you design web pages.

You should also not be a jerk, you would think this would be obvious but some people look at this and think “Of course I am not being a jerk to my users!” but then they add invasive popups, advertisements or cryptominers. And people accept their “masterpiece” as a “modern webpage”

If you haven’t heard of the Small Technology Foundation (Link), I highly suggest you read on their work (In fact, Oklomsy.com has signed and also complied with the Web 0 manifesto)

There are lots of initiatives, such as the 500KB club or Web 0, which want to make websites more technologically accessible, one using minimalism and the other is a petition for the webmaster to declare their site as technologically-accessible

The benefits of accessibility.

Technological accessibility is not neccessarily the same as an accessible design, quite often, technologically accessible webpages emphasize simplicity over structure and design while accessible designs often emphasize on complying with established standards rather than simplicity.

By keeping your website simple to begin with, it is accessible to all users, technological accessibility is more often than not also compliant with interface design standards or accessible design standards.

Your website will be lightning fast as there are no moving components, you just send a minimal webpage and that’s it, there are no hidden connections, no complicated rendering and no javascript engine would be needed thus putting less stress on browser developers and making the web more light than it was before.

Technological accessibility means creativity, if you want a unique design then get creative, learn how to achieve a design that is both awesome and technologically accessible.

As for Oklomsy.com

As for my personal website, I want to keep this website technologically accessible. It was designed with simplicity and conforming to every environment in mind, it was not designed to look pretty but I still think it is.

So. Here is absolutely required list of goals that I ensure with oklomsy.com (Your situation might be different, but feel free to adapt these)

  1. No javascript
  2. No external resources
  3. Every single webpage must not be larger than 1 MB
  4. No cookies
  5. Respect your user’s privacy
  6. The web page must be adaptable and responsive (Must fit all environments, such as phones, toasters and even ultra-wide screens.)

The Internet is for humans, not machines, so let’s work together to make every homepage technologically accessible.
